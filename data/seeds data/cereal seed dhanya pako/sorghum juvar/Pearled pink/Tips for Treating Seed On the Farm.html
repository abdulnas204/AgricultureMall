Tips for Treating Seed On the Farm

March 5, 2015 | Shannon Schindle
  • Protect
  • Winter 2015

Seed care experts offer up some best management practices to ensure your seed treatment delivers the expected value.

Seed treatments have become an invaluable tool for farmers, and industry analysts say it is more important than ever for seed companies and retailers to provide seed treatment stewardship.

According to Kerry Grossweiler, Bayer CropScience seedgrowth equipment and coatings manager, there are many aspects for farmers to consider when it comes to choosing the right seed treatment. “Producers should talk to an expert,” explains Grossweiler. “They need to visit their local seed dealer, ag retailer or Bayer representative and discuss on-farm pressures — whether that is disease or pests — and find the right product for their local conditions.”

Start Off Right

While seed treatments are important, so is seed cleaning and conditioning. “You are already investing in a high-value product for its traits and germplasm to provide high yield benefits,”  Grossweiler says. “Seed quality and conditioning are the important first step in the process.”

Bob Kolva, seed treatment manager with The McGregor Company agrees that seed conditioning and cleaning play a crucial role. “These processes remove foreign material, weed seed, damaged seed and small less vigorous seed and this is critical prior to applying a seed treatment,” Kolva explains.
Based out of Colfax, Washington, The McGregor Company provides growers in the Northwest with the seed, crop inputs, equipment, research and advice to raise healthy crops. The McGregor Company offers seed treatment products from Syngenta, Bayer CropScience, Novozymes, BASF and other leading companies to protect and enhance the yield potential of seeds for production agriculture.

Equipment

While the majority of seed is commercially treated, it’s important to ensure that any on-farm seed treating equipment is well maintained and in good working condition.

“Proper calibration is mission critical to accurate application,” Kolva says. “You need to measure both the flow of the seed through the treater as well as the amount of chemical applied to the seed. Proper chemical coverage is accomplished through a balance of seed flow and slurry rate applied to the seed.”
Grossweiler adds that it’s important to use the right equipment to achieve uniformity throughout the batch.

“This is why proper training of seed applicators helps to ensure safety and efficacy, not only for the environment but for the user as well,” he says.
Both Kolva and Grossweiler maintain that it is important to monitor the seed, keep samples throughout the treatment process and maintain detailed records of all applications.

Custom Formulas

The next step is to determine the proper application rate. This should be determined based on the label, local conditions and pest pressures. Kolva and Grossweiler recommend growers consult with a local seed care specialist for getting the right seed treatment formula.

“Proper recommendations vary greatly between regions and even within specific growing areas,” Kolva says. “Sales literature and Extension bulletins are great resources for background information but only that local consultant can provide specific prescriptions.”

One of the issues that impact application rate is seed size. Jeff Ohampaugh of Agrilead, Inc., says that ignoring seed size with respect to rate impacts success or failure. “For example, an under-dose fails to deliver the intended value and an over-dose is money out of your pocket,” he explains. “If we become more and more prescriptive — and I believe we will continue to go there — we must adjust the rate and application based on the size of the seed.

This is one reason why it’s important to consult an expert in this area. The McGregor Company formulates custom blends, which provide combinations of fungicides and insecticides specific to area micro-climates. Additionally, Grossweiler explains that Bayer CropScience representatives can also provide the right recipe for seed treatment success within regional areas throughout the Unites States.

Dust-reduction

With the onset of seed treatments becoming more widely used by farmers, there are also growing concerns about dust emissions during planting from treated seed — posing a risk to pollinators and the environment.

According to Kolva both dust-reduction and pollinator protection are huge issues not only for seed conditioners but also for farmers. “Both the farmer treater and the commercial treater need to consult with a seed care professional,” Kolva says. “Most crop consultants don’t have the time to be well versed in seed care issues.”

However, there are new products that are available to help reduce dust during planting and also be environmentally-friendly in the form of seed flow lubricants. Grossweiler explains that a seed flow lubricant helps to reduce seed-to-seed friction during the planting process reducing dust-off.

The value of seed care lubricants varies with the type of seed and the drills/planters that growers use.

However, Kolva adds that, “we feel the No. 1 one non-disease and insect performance issue facing growers is dust-off and the potential for off-target damage to farm workers and pollinators. We are continuously researching options for products that will help adhere seed treatments to the seed.”
Grossweiler explains that Bayer CropScience’s new Fluency Agent has proven itself in Canada and is set to provide growers in the United States with a tool that can reduce potential risk of exposure to dust-off.

According to data from Bayer, the company’s Fluency Agent reduces the amount of insecticide active ingredient released in treated seed dust during planting by 65 percent, reducing the risk of exposure to non-target insects, including honeybees.

“Bayer CropScience has worked with planter manufacturers and seed company partners in field trials,” Grossweiler says. “With the success we have seen in Canada, this new seed lubricant for corn and soybeans will help replace talc, graphite and talc/graphite blended seed lubricants to reduce
potential dust-off exposure for users and the environment.”

Bayer CropScience held large demonstration plots in the U.S. in 2014, and in Canada roughly 2 million acres of corn and 1 million acres of soybeans are expected to be planted with the Fluency Agent in 2015. Bayer expects to begin the promotion and launch of its Fluency Agent in the United States this growing season.
